# HYROX Training Malta: The 8 Stations and How to Train for Each

HYROX is a fixed-format fitness race. Every event, anywhere in the world, is the same: eight rounds of a 1km run followed by one workout station. The running adds up to 8km. The stations test strength, grip, core and lung capacity in that order of brutality. If you are looking at HYROX training in Malta, the work is to build specific capacity at each of those eight stations while keeping your 1km run pace honest under fatigue.

This guide walks through every station, what it actually feels like at race pace, and how we prep for it at [F15 Training Centr in Msida](/). Use it as a map for your own training, or come in and train the stations on the floor they were built for.

## The Running Between Stations Is the Real Test

Before the stations, a note on the runs. Eight 1km efforts sounds manageable. It is not. By round five, your quads are full of lactate from sled work and lunges, and your shoulders are cooked from wall balls and ski erg. The target is not your fresh 1km pace. It is your repeatable pace after a station.

In HYROX prep Malta sessions at F15 Training Centr, we build this in two ways. Short intervals, 400m to 1km on the treadmill or outdoors, paired with a station finisher. And longer compromised-pace blocks where you run five or six kilometres with work in between. The aim is a run pace that does not fall off a cliff when your legs are trashed.

## Station 1: 1000m Ski Erg

The ski erg opens the race. Cold muscles, high heart rate from the first run, and a machine that punishes anyone who pulls with their arms. The fix is hip hinge mechanics. Drive from the hips, let the arms follow, and keep a steady stroke rate around 30 to 34 per minute.

Train it with intervals: 8 rounds of 250m at race pace, 45 seconds rest. Once a week, do a 1000m time trial fresh and another after a 1km run. The gap between those two numbers tells you how well your engine holds up.

## Station 2: 50m Sled Push

This is where HYROX becomes a leg day. The men's sled is 152kg gross on a rubber floor, the women's is 102kg. It does not roll. It grinds.

Sled push training in Malta is genuinely hard to replicate outside a proper functional gym. You need a long flat run, a heavy prowler, and a surface that mimics race conditions. We have all three at the Msida box, and the Paola location will have the same when it opens. Key points: hands low, arms straight, hips low, and take short aggressive steps. Do not stand tall and push with your chest.

Progressions: start with 4 x 25m unbroken at a lighter load, build to 2 x 50m unbroken at race weight. If you have to stop more than twice in 50m on race day, your base strength needs work before your conditioning does.

## Station 3: 50m Sled Pull

Rope, heavy sled, 50m in two 25m pulls because of the course layout. Grip fails first, then lats, then legs. The technique that saves time is a seated hip-drive: low stance, pull hand over hand, and use the legs to reset between pulls rather than trying to muscle it with the upper back.

Accessory work that transfers: heavy rows, farmer holds, and reverse sled drags for time. Twice a week is enough.

## Station 4: 80m Burpee Broad Jump

Every burpee, every jump, for 80 metres. No run, no reset, just rep after rep in a narrow lane. Most athletes blow up here because they go too hard in the first 20m.

Practise pacing with a tempo: chest to floor, stand, small committed jump forward, land balanced. Aim for a jump distance you can repeat 40 times, not the biggest one you can hit on rep three. Training set: 4 x 20m burpee broad jumps with 90 seconds rest.

## Station 5: 1000m Row

Coming off the burpees, the row feels like a recovery. It is not. Most athletes lose 15 to 30 seconds here by dropping stroke rate too low.

Target a 2:00 to 2:15 per 500m pace depending on your level, stroke rate around 26 to 30. Keep the drive strong and the recovery slow. Train it with 6 x 500m intervals at goal pace, 1 minute rest. Then test 1000m after a 1km run, twice a month.

## Station 6: 200m Farmers Carry

24kg kettlebells each hand for men, 16kg for women. 200m. Grip is the bottleneck for most people, not legs. Walk tall, brace the core, and take quick short steps.

Build capacity with timed holds (3 x 60 seconds at race weight) and carries of 100m to 200m. Mix in a heavier carry once a week, 28kg or 32kg per hand for 50m, to make race weight feel lighter.

## Station 7: 100m Sandbag Lunges

20kg sandbag for men, 10kg for women, across the shoulders, 100m of walking lunges. This is usually the hardest station mentally. Quads are gone from the sled and the runs, and 100m of lunges is a lot of reps.

The trick is to never stop moving. Short stride, knee to the floor softly, stand, next leg. If you stop, the lactate catches up and restarting costs more than slowing down. Train it in 25m blocks with short rests, then build to 50m unbroken, then the full 100m.

## Station 8: 100 Wall Balls

The final station. Wall ball HYROX reps are 9kg for men to a 3m target, 6kg for women to 2.7m. One hundred reps. Legs are destroyed, shoulders are burning, and the room is spinning.

Break it early. Most sub-elite athletes do better with sets of 20 or 25 from the start than trying to hold 50 unbroken and blowing up at rep 35. Train unbroken sets of 30 to 40 twice a week, and test 100 for time weekly. Your breathing pattern matters: exhale on the throw, inhale on the catch.
